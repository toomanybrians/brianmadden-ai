# Liquidware Chats - March 31, 2026

## Episode details

**Title:** You Can't Automate Your Way to Intelligence

**Context:** Brian's first appearance on Liquidware Chats, hosted by James O'Regan (Liquidware Product Marketing Manager). The conversation covers the full arc from VDI Delusion parallels through the cognitive stack to practical advice for enterprises. This is Brian's most complete public articulation of the cognitive stack framework and the "you can't automate your way to intelligence" thesis.

**Key arguments made:**
- VDI Delusion parallel: the same pattern is playing out with AI. Workers want to work their way, corporations want to control them. The need is real, the solutions are often wrong.
- Brian uses AI as a cognitive extension: 90% of his computer interaction is through AI. Microsoft Word is "just a compatibility layer" for colleagues who haven't adopted this approach yet.
- Second brain mechanics: markdown files in a folder, Claude Code, everything fed in. Ideas get mined, connected, and tracked automatically.
- Predicted cognitive extension for 2027+ in the 7-stage roadmap (July 2025), it happened by January 2026: wrong by over a year
- The 80/20 problem: 80% of knowledge work is invisible (inside workers' heads), companies can only see 20%. Corporate AI only targets the visible 20%, which is why ROI disappoints.
- Individual workers with $20/month subscriptions say AI "fundamentally changed the way I work" because they're applying AI to the invisible 80%.
- The cognitive stack (5 layers): worker at top, cognitive extension, skills, agents, interfaces/workflows at bottom. Like the OSI model: intelligence lives at the top, infrastructure at the bottom.
- "You will never automate your way to super intelligence": automations are layer 2 switches. Important, but no amount of layer 2 intelligence creates an application delivery controller.
- Applications becoming compatibility layers: Word, Teams, email, to-do lists are "old compatibility layers." Already sharing work with colleagues via GitHub submodules between second brains.
- Software development as canary: what happened in November 2025 to software development is coming for all knowledge work. Went from skeptics to "I don't write code anymore" in two weeks.
- Security implications: tokens instead of packets, MCP data sources instead of applications, DLP can't work on regex when dealing with cognitive content
- Infrastructure still essential: the 80% collapses without a solid foundation. But recognize it as foundation, not endgame.
- "Slow then fast": everything with AI happens slowly until it doesn't. "I don't think you have two years."

## Condensed Transcript

James opens by referencing Brian's 2012 book *The VDI Delusion* and asks whether AI is the new VDI.

Brian draws the parallel: VDI was aspirationally solving the right problems (work from anywhere, stay secure) but delivering the wrong solution. Workers wanted to work their way; the company wanted control. That tension is replaying with AI. Some companies try to limit workers to what their technology can do today, just like VDI vendors did. But it doesn't have to be that way.

On his day-to-day role, Brian explains he works remotely and 90% of his computer interaction is through AI. He uses it as a cognitive extension: every meeting gets mined, every idea gets tracked, voice recordings while walking the dog get transcribed and connected to existing threads. He points AI at a folder on his hard drive with everything he's published, and it mines out core insights, tracks story arcs, rough ideas, and vision concepts. "So now every presentation or every question that comes in, I'm able to sort of call on all of that context."

On time horizons, Brian admits his 7-stage roadmap article from July 2025 projected cognitive extension use at "2027 plus," but he was doing it by January 2026. "So six months ago, what I projected would be like two and a half years plus turned into less than six months." His actual horizon is about two years, but he can't be sure whether the steps he sees are five years of steps or two months of steps.

James asks where AI fits in EUC specifically. Brian introduces the 80/20 framework: 80% of knowledge work happens inside workers' heads (invisible to the company), while companies can only see the 20% (emails, documents, meetings). Corporate AI initiatives show disappointing ROI because they're only targeting that visible 20%. Meanwhile, individual workers applying AI to the invisible 80% are seeing transformative results.

Brian then introduces the cognitive stack. Five layers, like the OSI model: worker at the top, cognitive extension below, then skills, agents, and interfaces/workflows at the bottom. The 80% lives at the top of the stack. The bottom layers (where all of EUC currently operates) are the visible 20%. "You can have the smartest automation layer in the world and it's not going to sprout up value intelligence out of the top."

On applications as compatibility layers: Brian no longer uses Microsoft Word unless interfacing with colleagues who haven't adopted second brains. He and colleagues share work through GitHub submodules between their second brains. "Teams is an old compatibility layer. Word is an old compatibility layer. To-do lists are an old compatibility layer. Pretty much all of Microsoft Office is an old compatibility layer."

On security: when the previously invisible 80% gets digitized through AI cognitive extensions, the security model changes fundamentally. "We're talking about tokens instead of packets. We're talking about MCP data sources. DLP changes completely because you can't do DLP rules based on regex and scripts when you're looking at cognitive things."

On pace of change: Brian points to software development as the canary. When Claude 4.5, Gemini 3, and ChatGPT 5.3 came out in November, the entire coding world changed in two weeks. "I've always looked at software development as a canary in the coal mine for what's happening in knowledge work." The same thing is coming for all knowledge work, and it will be "slow then fast."

Brian's advice for enterprises: start by using AI as a cognitive extension yourself. "The first thing you have to do is just start using it and just understand like, whoa, this is really possible today." Then think through the cognitive stack and understand where value actually lives. The infrastructure foundation must be solid (secure endpoints, good performance), but recognize that automating the bottom of the stack is building the foundation, not the endgame. "You're not automating your way to super intelligence. Like, that's just not how it works."
