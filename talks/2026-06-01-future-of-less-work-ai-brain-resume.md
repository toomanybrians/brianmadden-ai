# The Future of Less Work — How AI is changing who performs best at work

## Episode details

**Context:** Guest appearance on Nirit Cohen's podcast, which explores how work transforms as AI enters. Cohen opens with the framing: the lines between employee-owned and employer-owned used to be clear—everything you did at work belonged to your employer. AI second brains blur that line in both directions. The conversation covers what happens when capability shifts from individuals to human-AI combinations, who owns the brain a worker builds, and why productivity is the wrong metric for any of it.

**Key arguments made:**

- The new unit of work is human + AI system. The individual is no longer the unit; it's the human plus the intelligence they've built around themselves and are able to orchestrate.
- Workers have been building personal AI systems since ChatGPT launched—first for entertainment, then for work, until within months the AI knows more about their working environment than their teams or boss does.
- BYOAI parallels BYOD: same pattern, but structurally different. Corporate plans don't have unlimited use like personal subscriptions. More important: the AI accumulates deeply personal working context that workers don't want to leave behind.
- The inverse labor threat: not "can I take my brain when I leave?" but "can the company keep my brain after they fire me?" AI finally captures what was previously invisible—judgment, pattern recognition, mullings—that always lived only in workers' heads.
- Invisible knowledge work: documents and emails are artifacts of knowledge work, not knowledge work itself. Most AI systems only target that visible layer. AI as co-thinking partner operates on the invisible 80%.
- Brain-to-brain connections are already live: colleagues with wired second brains communicate through them, bypassing email and to-do lists.
- Manage AI like a worker, not a technology project: its own login ID, restricted permissions, trained with skills documents. The things people say AI can't handle (forgetting, going down, inconsistency) apply equally to humans.
- Joy/satisfaction as the individual calibration metric: use AI to figure out what parts of your job bring joy and outsource the rest. The question isn't more productivity—it's more of what matters.
- Timeline compression: predictions that looked like 2027+ were live in production by January 2026.
- Productivity is a company metric, not a worker metric. Individuals don't care about throughput—they care about satisfaction and agency.

## Condensed transcript

Nirit opens with the framing: when we use AI tools, we're building an extension of ourselves—a personal operating system for thinking. That capability is becoming a real differentiator, shifting the definition of what "resources" are. The unit of work is no longer the individual—it's the human plus the intelligence they've built around them and are able to orchestrate.

**On where the human + AI system is already taking shape:**

Individual workers have been finding and using AI tools on their own since ChatGPT launched. The workers on the frontier have moved beyond using it as a simple answer engine. They're using it to manage all the context for everything they need to do at work. At first it's sports scores and birthday poems. Within two to four months, the AI knows more about your working environment than maybe your teams do, or your boss, or your inbox.

**On the BYOAI parallel to BYOD:**

There was a time 15 years ago when iPhones came out and individual workers had better technology than their companies. People just used their own devices. The way companies solved BYOD was to offer their own versions—Microsoft got better, Google got better, workers got to work the way they wanted and companies could manage it. This time it seems similar at first: buy your own AI subscription, use it for work. But there's a structural difference. Consumer plans have unlimited use; corporate plans don't. And the AI you build accumulates context that feels deeply personal—workers don't want to leave it behind.

**On who owns the AI brain you build:**

Nirit pushes the IP question: not just the data, but the fact that you're building tools that take your thinking further. Who owns that when you leave?

The real worry is the inverse. I've been using AI in this personal way since January of this year. Within two days it changed how I worked. If I left my job tomorrow and couldn't take it with me, honestly, it wouldn't matter—I'd start fresh and within two days I'd have a new second brain. But the real worry is the company saying: "Hey, you've sufficiently populated your little personal Wikipedia. Now we can fire you and we keep your brain."

That's always happened with documents and emails. The big difference with AI is that so much of knowledge work is what happens inside your head—it's invisible. Documents and emails are not actual knowledge work; they're the artifacts of knowledge work. Most AI systems today focus on those artifacts. But if you can get AI to be a co-thinking partner—capturing your judgment, pattern recognition, mullings—then you start affecting the level that actually matters. You could point an AI at someone's entire working knowledge repo and have an AI-powered interface to them even when they're not there.

**On managing AI like a worker:**

Whether you want AI to happen, your vote doesn't count. It's happening. The way AI has failed in corporations is when companies treat it like a traditional technology project—evaluate it, work with consulting firms, read the analysts, plug it together. You need to think of AI more like a worker. Not because it's conscious or has a soul—it isn't and it doesn't. But in terms of how you implement it: it needs its own login ID. I might want it to read my email but only read, not send. I might want it to read the CRM but not write to it. And you train AI the same way you train humans—with skills documents. When people say "what if AI hallucinates, what if it goes down?"—all that applies to humans too. And we figured it out.

**On the subscribable brains model:**

I've made a public version of my second brain—all my writings, thinking, and synthesis—online and free, so anyone can connect their AI to it. As a consultant model, imagine leaving a living, working brain behind in an organization after an engagement. Maybe they pay you per month to access it. The knowledge distribution model inverts: instead of workshops and deliverables, the deliverable is the knowledge system itself. In the way you could look at this as a downside for workers—in the consulting world, it's a game changer.

**On joy as the calibration metric:**

I'm at the phase of my life where I'm asking: what do I want to do? Not at the macro level—consultant, engineer, CTO—but at the task level. What parts of my job bring me joy and satisfaction, and what parts take energy from me in ways that aren't healthy? I've recalibrated: the things I've outsourced to AI are the things that drained me. The things I do myself are what make me truly happy—like doing a podcast with an actual human. The question is: what brings you joy at the task level? Once you know that, maximize it. Minimize everything else.

Individuals don't care about productivity. Companies do. Everyone's AI system is so different because everyone has built theirs to solve their specific problems. Someone's hard part is someone else's fun part. The real question isn't "do I do more of the same so I get more productivity?" It's "what does this free me up to do?"
