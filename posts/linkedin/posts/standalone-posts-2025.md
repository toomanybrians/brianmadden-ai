"Knowledge worker automation" is absurd, right??

All the AI vendors are pushing "AI powered automation studios", which are cool, but also I think too many people are making too much of a big deal about them.

These are a point-in-time stepping stone solution, NOT the be-all, end-all to how AI will help knowledge workers.

For one, knowledge worker tasks are (by definition), varied, random, and different every day. (If they did the same thing every day, we would call them "task workers". 😜)

Also, using these AI automation studios requires people to stop working and spend time trying to learn, study, figure out, etc. Who has time for that? This is why "learn about AI automations" has been on peoples' to-do lists for 12 months.

So, yeah, maybe a few % of knowledge workers build some automations, but 95%+ will not and will experience AI when it's more able to meet them where they are.

https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:share:7406319024105598976

It's official! The leading AI computing using agent (CUA) now scores higher than humans in the OSWorld benchmark for real-world tasks in computer environments.

Simular Research Agent S3: 72.6%
Humans: 72.4%

Six months ago, the best AI agents scored 45%. A year ago it was 17%, and eighteen months ago, the best scored 12%.

I've written quite a bit on why computer-using agents will be huge for enterprise knowledge workers, and how it's inevitable that they'll use the same desktops, apps, and workspaces as human workers. The question was always "when," not "if."

To be clear, hitting human parity on a benchmark doesn't mean CUAs are ready to replace workers now. The benchmark tests raw UI execution—can the agent click the right buttons, navigate menus, and complete task based on plain-language instructions? It doesn't test judgment, context, knowing why to do something, or handling the weird edge cases that fill every real workday.

But that's exactly the point. The "hands and eyes" part of computer work is pretty much solved. What remains is the orchestration & scaffolding that tells agents what to do, when, and with what guardrails.

For enterprise IT, this changes the conversation. It's no longer about whether AI can operate your legacy apps. It can. Now it's about where those agents will run, how you'll secure them, and who provides the oversight.

The future interface for ALL knowledge work is going to be AI. Full stop.

Not an AI-powered app. Not copilots embedded in your existing tools. I'm saying the AI platform itself will become the primary way you interact with your apps, data, and work environment. Everything else will become infrastructure that AI operates on your behalf.

I know that sounds wacky. So I wrote a post walking through exactly how we get there (within the constraints of your existing enterprise systems, not some fantasy where everything gets rebuilt as AI-native).

This is my second cornerstone post. It's long (2k words), but worth it, as it lays out (along with my 7-stages of human-AI collaboration) how AI will enter the enterprise workforce over the next few years.

The short version:

AI platforms (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) already have four pathways to reach everything in your environment: connectors to modern apps, browser control for web apps, computer-using agents for legacy desktop apps, and direct file manipulation that skip the apps entirely.

Workers won't consciously "choose AI over apps." They'll just notice one day that they haven't opened Excel in two months. 🤷‍♂️ The shift will happen through accumulated trust over time, not a point-in-time decision.

And no, this doesn't mean everything becomes AI slop. AI will handle the brokering and navigation, but you'll still bring judgment to the actual work.

I've been building toward this piece for months. It ties together the 7-stage roadmap, the CUA benchmarks, the AI file manipulation stuff, and the "boring infrastructure" concepts into a single thesis.

Doing this right will be a lot of work, and not without challenges. But the payoff is immense and the work is worth doing. There are plenty of steps companies can take today.

None of that is in those open weights models. So while it’s a fine move, it doesn’t mean individuals or companies can simply swap it in and have the full ChatGPT experience on their own infrastructure.

What does the "workspace" mean in 2025? I know... I KNOW!! Right?? "Another vendor blog about what is a workspace." 😴🙄 Barf! 🤢🤮

Even though the desktop has dissolved, we've still traditionally thought of the workspace as a "thing" that exists (just in different forms: desktop, browser, mobile, etc.) But thinking of the workspace as a noun doesn't help with the hard questions of today:

• How do we secure something that doesn’t really live anywhere anymore?
• How do we manage it when it’s everywhere and nowhere at the same time?
• How do we control access if everything is now a service instead of a thing?

The modern workspace is a fungible abstraction of a location where work can happen. It's instantiated via devices and techniques we're well aware of: a VDI desktop, a MacBook with Chrome, a mobile app, a browser tab. It’s in any and all and none of these at the same time. But those tangible representations of a workspace are just that—instantiated representations of the workspace—not the workspace itself!

So what is a workspace? Sure, its ingredients are apps & data, identity, security, context... And anywhere and time those converge, the potential is there. The workspace is a dynamic control plane, ready to do work, but idle. 

The moment a worker connects—bringing their knowledge, intent, and agency—is when the workspace becomes real. (It’s alive!)

This perspective shift matters because very soon, the notion of a worker is going to change drastically. While it's traditionally been human, soon it will be human, machine, or a combination of both. Organizations need to figure out how to manage, control, and enable this, which requires this mental model shift.

The reward for making this shift is you'll be ready to support all types of work regardless of the worker. Because in 2025 and beyond, work no longer lives in a fixed location or even on a collections of devices—it emerges dynamically, wherever a worker connects to a workspace.

I unpack this fully in my latest blog post: https://lnkd.in/gcCyvE65

I just joined Citrix!

Those who know me know I’ve spent my 30-year career orbiting Citrix. From the early years (last century!) when I worked with WinFrame & MetaFrame, lived on the THIN list, and earned my CCEA; to writing books on Citrix, VDI, and DaaS; to creating my blog, my independent Citrix master class, and the BriForum conference (a nod to Citrix’s own iForum conference name), I’ve spent decades writing, speaking, and debating about VDI, DaaS, EUC and the future of work.

Yet despite all that, I never actually worked for Citrix.

Until now.

Today, I’m thrilled to share that I’ve joined Citrix as a full-time employee! (I’ll continue to be based in Paris.)

So, why Citrix? And why now?

Quite simply, there’s no more exciting place to be.

Citrix has always been about the future of work. Even though we didn’t call it that in the ‘90s, I first saw that future when I did a complex update to an Oracle client on a WinFrame server that was instantly available to 15(!) employees. My mind was further blown when I realized the app performed the same for branch office workers 1000 miles away over a T1.

I remember that moment (at Telxon, in Akron, Ohio) like it was yesterday, and it changed everything for me.

I quickly embraced Citrix as my primary focus, and it was the topic of my first-ever public talk in August 1998. Back then, we talked about the benefits as “MAPS” (management, access, performance, and security). That evolved into the “any, any, any” (anywhere, anytime, any device) era as Citrix began to fundamentally transformation in how people worked.

Over the coming decades, my answer to “What would you say you do here?” evolved: SBC, ASP, VDI, DaaS, UEM, COIT, EUC, Digital Workspace, Workplace AI… Each of these was part of the bigger story of how work gets done.

Citrix evolved too, though their foundation has always been about securing the work—regardless of the worker, app, or platform—fundamentals as important today as they were 30 years ago.

If you’ve been following my posts & talks over the past few years, you know I believe we’re in the midst of the biggest transformation of work in our lifetimes. Companies will need flexibility and adaptability to support more types of workers, on more platforms, using more applications than ever before. They’ll need visibility and control. They’ll need to simplify TCO. And above all, they’ll need to secure the work, whatever that looks like in the future.

This is why I’m here.

Joining Citrix isn’t just a job for me—it’s the culmination of everything I’ve worked on for decades. Citrix has an incredible story to tell, and I’m honored to step into a role where I can help tell that story and shape its future.

The future of work isn’t just a single moment or technology—it’s an ongoing movement we’ve all been part of for decades. Thirty years from now, we’ll laughingly look back at this moment like it’s a 1990s T1, but sitting here today, I couldn’t be more excited about what’s ahead.

Let’s go!
